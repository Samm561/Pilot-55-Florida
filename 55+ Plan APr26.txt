A practical Onboard60 architecture for a pilot 55+ community in Florida can be built in three phases: registry, HOA governance, then equity/utility tokens, all on a single primary chain plus an off‑chain data layer.

1. Base tech stack (Florida pilot)

Primary chain:
  Use a permissioned EVM chain (e.g., Polygon Supernet / OP Stack‑style rollup or a managed consortium chain) so you control fees, privacy, and governance, while staying Solidity‑compatible for tooling and audits.

Identity & access:
  Residents and family get non‑custodial wallets wrapped with email/phone login (e.g., Web3Auth‑style UX), plus KYC done once and anchored on‑chain as a verified role (owner, resident, caregiver, vendor).

Off‑chain 
  Use a conventional database (e.g., Postgres) and file storage (e.g., S3) for PHI‑adjacent and sensitive documents; only hashes and minimal pointers live on‑chain to avoid HIPAA and privacy problems.

2. Phase 1 : Onboard60 Registry Layer

Goal: A canonical, tamper‑evident registry for units, members, and rights (not money yet).

Core smart contracts:
  Unit Registry Contract: Each unit (lot, condo, home) is an NFT‑like record with metadata (address, type, occupancy status, permitted resident count, accessibility features).
  Membership / Occupancy Contract: Maps people to units with roles: Owner, Life‑Lease Holder, Renter, Family Contact, Caregiver; tracks start/stop dates and reason for change (move‑in, care level change, death, transfer).

Admin roles:
  “Registrar” (Onboard60 or property manager) is analogous to the land‑inspector role from blockchain registry patterns; can approve transfers, move‑ins, and corrections under defined rules.

Resident UX:
   Simple portal: “My Home,” “My Household,” “My Contacts” pulling from smart contracts; residents never see raw transaction details unless they choose to.  

What this unlocks:
Single source of truth for “who lives where” with an immutable history, which future HOA logic and equity tokenization can trust.

3. Phase 2 : Smart‑contract HOA / governance

Goal: Turn CC&Rs, dues, and board actions into transparent, parameterized smart contracts.

Token type 1 : Governance token (non‑transferable HOA voting units):
  Issue soulbound governance tokens (one per unit, or proportional to square footage) to the wallet(s) designated as “current owner/occupant” by the registry.
Tokens automatically move when occupancy/ownership changes in the registry, avoiding “lost” votes.  

HOA smart contracts:
Assessment Contract: Stores base dues per unit type, special assessments, due dates, and penalty logic; emits events for each billing cycle that the off‑chain billing system mirrors.
  Governance / Voting Contract: Supports proposals (rule changes, budget approvals, capital projects), quorum thresholds, and weighted voting by governance tokens; board vs. community‑wide proposals use different thresholds.
  Rules & Exceptions Contract:Encodes “soft” logic (e.g., noise rules, parking), but allows recorded board overrides for humanitarian or medical reasons (important in 55+ contexts), all auditable.

Florida adaptation:
  Align proposal and notice periods with Florida HOA/condo statutes; smart contracts store timestamps and resolution records to help prove statutory compliance in disputes.

What this unlocks:
Transparent history of dues changes, rule votes, and board decisions, which is powerful for trust‑building with seniors and their families.

4. Phase 3 – Tokenized equity & utility for a 55+ community

Goal: Add investment and service layers without overburdening residents.

Token type 2 : Equity / “Community Share” token (security):
  Use a Tokeny class or similar RWA engine to issue “security tokens” representing equity in the campus or in a dedicated property‑owning entity, with full KYC/AML and transfer‑restriction logic.
Investor categories: developer/fund, family members, local impact investors, potentially residents (subject to suitability rules and U.S. securities exemptions).
  Distributions: rent or “community surplus” flows off‑chain, with on‑chain records of declared distributions and each token holder’s entitlement.  
Token type 3 Utility / service credits:
 Non‑security  “utility tokens” or off‑chain points representing prepaid or earned benefits: transportation vouchers, wellness classes, guest‑suite nights, tech‑support hours. 

  These are not meant to appreciate in value but to standardize and track service delivery, and could be partially auto‑granted to equity token holders as a “member benefit.”  

Risk controls for seniors:
Governance tokens are non‑transferable and do not equal financial equity.  
Equity tokens can be limited to family or external investors, with clear disclaimers and optional “resident share” programs capped at modest amounts.
Utility tokens never function as payment for rent or medical services, avoiding regulatory confusion.  

5. Phased rollout plan in practice

1. Year 1 Registry MVP (single property in Florida):
   Implement registry contracts and resident portal.  
   Integrate with property‑management software (payments, maintenance tickets) using on‑chain IDs, but keep money flows conventional.  
2. Year 2 – HOA governance pilot:
   Roll out governance tokens and basic voting (amenities hours, small rules) before touching dues/budgets.  
   Gradually move assessments and budget approvals on‑chain once residents are comfortable and legal counsel signs off.  
3. Year 3 – Equity + utility layer:
   Launch a Reg D/Reg CF‑style offering of equity tokens in a new Onboard60‑branded community or expansion phase, using a compliant tokenization provider.
   Add utility credits for wellness/transport as a “soft” loyalty and engagement layer.
